Warm-up: Card Probabilities

A deck of cards. A standard deck has 52 cards. If we shuffle well and draw one card, each of the 52 cards is equally likely. That’s our starting point: equally likely configurations.

One Card

Single Card Probabilities

You draw one card from a well-shuffled deck.

  1. What is the probability of drawing a heart?
  2. What is the probability of drawing a 5?
  3. What is the probability of drawing a face card (J, Q, or K)?

Each of the 52 cards is equally likely, so we just count.

  1. There are 13 hearts, so \(P(\text{heart}) = 13/52 = 1/4\).
  2. There are 4 fives (one per suit), so \(P(\text{five}) = 4/52 = 1/13\).
  3. There are 12 face cards (3 per suit × 4 suits), so \(P(\text{face card}) = 12/52 = 3/13\).

Two Cards with Replacement

Now suppose we draw two cards with replacement—we draw one, note it, put it back, shuffle, and draw again. Each draw is equally likely to be any of the 52 cards, so there are \(52 \times 52 = 2704\) equally likely pairs.

Two Cards with Replacement

You draw two cards with replacement from a well-shuffled deck.

  1. What is the probability that both cards are hearts?
  2. What is the probability that both cards have the same suit?
  3. What is the probability of drawing a pair (two cards of the same rank)?

We count configurations.

  1. There are \(13 \times 13 = 169\) ways to draw two hearts, so \(P(\text{both hearts}) = 169/2704 = 1/16\).

  2. There are 4 suits, and for each suit there are \(13 \times 13 = 169\) ways to draw two cards from that suit. So there are \(4 \times 169 = 676\) same-suit pairs, giving \(P(\text{same suit}) = 676/2704 = 1/4\).

  3. There are 13 ranks. For each rank, there are \(4 \times 4 = 16\) ways to draw two cards of that rank (4 choices for the first card’s suit, 4 for the second). So there are \(13 \times 16 = 208\) pairs, giving \(P(\text{pair}) = 208/2704 = 1/13\).

Thinking about Marginalization

Notice what we did in the “same suit” calculation. We could have listed all 2704 pairs and counted which ones had matching suits. Instead, we grouped them: 676 pairs have matching suits (169 for each of the 4 suits), and the rest don’t. This grouping—summing probabilities over configurations that share some property—is marginalization. We’ll use it constantly.
